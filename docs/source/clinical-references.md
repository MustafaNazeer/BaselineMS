# Clinical references

This file is the annotated source bibliography for every test in the battery and every validation target. Each entry names the reference, summarizes its relevance, and (where applicable) cites the specific number or methodology used in this project.

Citations follow the format used in clinical literature reviews (author year; journal). DOIs are listed where available. The Clinical Validator is responsible for keeping this file accurate; the PM only adds entries on the Clinical Validator's instruction.

## Disease and assessment baseline

### Multiple Sclerosis epidemiology
- Walton et al. 2020, *Multiple Sclerosis Journal*. Global prevalence approximately 2.8 million as of 2020. Used in `SPEC.md` Section 2.

### Expanded Disability Status Scale (EDSS)
- Kurtzke 1983, *Neurology*. The EDSS itself, the standard clinical disability scale in MS. Administered by neurologists in clinic at three to six month intervals. The application aims to fill the gap between EDSS visits with weekly digital measurements.

### FLOODLIGHT (Roche / Genentech research program)
- Montalban et al. 2021, *npj Digital Medicine*, "Smartphone-based remote assessment of MS." The original Floodlight precedent. Research only, locked to specific studies. The BaselineMS aims to be the open community equivalent. Cited in `SPEC.md` Sections 1 and 2.
- Oh et al. 2024, *Scientific Reports*, "Floodlight Open, an open access global longitudinal smartphone based study of multiple sclerosis: Cohort characteristics and study design." DOI 10.1038/s41598-023-49299-4. Analysis of 1,350 self declared MS plus 1,133 non MS participants from 17 countries, 2018 to 2021. Two findings load bearing for this project:
  - **4 of 7 active tests significantly discriminated MS from non MS after age and sex adjustment**: Information Processing Speed Test (p < 0.001), IPS Digit Digit Test (p < 0.001), Pinching Test (p < 0.001), U Turn Test (p < 0.05). The 3 that did not discriminate after adjustment: Draw a Shape Test, Static Balance Test, and Two Minute Walk Test (2MWT).
  - **The 2MWT did not significantly distinguish MS from non MS in this cohort.** This finding supports the BaselineMS design choice to compute richer per stride gait features (stride length, cadence, asymmetry, step time variability, double support time) from a 30 second walk rather than reporting only a global distance metric. Cited in `SPEC.md` Section 7.
- Galati et al. 2024, *JMIR Human Factors* 11:e57033, "User Experience of a Large-Scale Smartphone-Based Observational Study in Multiple Sclerosis: Global, Open-Access, Digital-Only Study." DOI 10.2196/57033. The user experience companion paper to Oh 2024. Three findings load bearing for this project:
  - **Reminders roughly triple day 30 retention.** US MS cohort, daily assessment series: with reminders enabled (n=172) day 1 65.7%, day 7 32.6%, day 14 30.8%, day 30 30.8%. Without reminders (n=350) day 30 fell to 9.7%. This is the empirical retention floor for this population.
  - **Onboarding redesign moved registration to activation conversion from 53.9% to 74.6%**, a 20 point retention floor that exists before any test runs. Onboarding deserves Phase 0 attention.
  - **Qualitative quit reasons** (Table 4) clustered into: activation and onboarding confusion, forgetting without reminders, competing life demands, physical or contextual barriers (including unsafe locations for the gait test), test burden and repetitiveness, instruction comprehension, and 2MWT scheduling friction specifically. The 2MWT had the lowest day 1 retention of any test (32.34% without reminders, 40.7% with).
- **Used in:** `SPEC.md` Sections 7, 9, 16; `docs/plan.md` Phase 0; `agents/04-ui-ux-designer.md` Phase 0.

## Test specific references

### Bilateral Tap Test (proxy for 9 Hole Peg Test)
- Mathiowetz V, Weber K, Kashman N, Volland G. 1985, *Occupational Therapy Journal of Research* 5(1):24 to 38. DOI 10.1177/153944928500500102. The original 9HPT (Adult Norms for the Nine Hole Peg Test of Finger Dexterity). Standard EDSS upper limb measure. (Journal name corrected per Citation Auditor Phase 0 verdict A.6, which identified the original entry as misattributed to *American Journal of Occupational Therapy*.)
- Tanigawa M, Stein J, Park J, Kosa P, Cortese I, Bielekova B. 2017, *Multiple Sclerosis Journal: Experimental, Translational and Clinical* 3(1):2055217316688930. DOI 10.1177/2055217316688930. PMID 28680701. Investigates finger and foot tapping as smartphone adaptable alternatives to the 9HPT and the T25FW in MS. Reports that finger and foot tapping differentiate relapsing remitting MS from progressive MS in a cross sectional sample and detect one year and two year disease progression with greater statistical power (z scores) than the 9HPT and T25FW. Provides direct support for using a smartphone tap test as a 9HPT alternative in MS, replacing the previously cited (and unlocatable) "Bays et al. 2015" reference per Citation Auditor Phase 0 verdict A.7. Caveat: Tanigawa 2017 used a desktop or web based tap interface, not the specific bilateral alternating tap on two on screen touch targets that this project implements; Phase 2 should treat the design as inspired by the same construct (rapid tap rate as an upper limb dexterity measure in MS) rather than as a one to one replication. The MSOAC consensus paper Feys et al. 2017, *Multiple Sclerosis Journal* 23(5):711 to 720, DOI 10.1177/1352458517690824, sits alongside Mathiowetz 1985 as the modern MS specific 9HPT clinical trial endpoint reference.
- **Used in:** Phase 2 Tap Test design.

### Gait analysis on smartphone IMU
- Bea T, Chaabene H, Freitag CW, Schega L. 2025, *Journal of Functional Morphology and Kinesiology* 10(2):133. DOI 10.3390/jfmk10020133. PMID 40566429. Open access systematic review of psychometric characteristics of smartphone based gait analyses in chronic health conditions (54 studies, including MS, Parkinson's, stroke, and other neurological populations). Validity for spatial parameters such as stride length and step length against gold standard systems (e.g., Vicon, GAITRite) reported as r = 0.42 to 0.90, with temporal parameters (gait speed, cadence) showing higher r = 0.74 to 0.97. Test retest reliability for spatiotemporal parameters ICC = 0.53 to 0.95, with hip and pocket placements ICC = 0.85 to 0.95. Standard error of measurement (SEM) for spatiotemporal parameters, where reported (in only 2 of 54 studies, per the Citation Auditor's Phase 0 to Phase 1 cleanup re audit verifying the verbatim quote against the open access PMC full text), ranged from 2.1 percent to 7.8 percent of the walking distance, which approximately brackets the project's 5 percent stride length target. Replaces the previously cited (and unlocatable) "Buckley et al. 2020 *Sensors*" entry per Citation Auditor Phase 0 verdict A.8. Caveat from the Clinical Validator: SEM was reported in only two of the 54 included studies, so the 2.1 percent to 7.8 percent range is a thin literature anchor; the project's 5 percent target is also defended by its own Phase 5 validation experiment against a measured walking course, which remains the primary justification.
- Madgwick 2010, "An efficient orientation filter for inertial and inertial/magnetic sensor arrays." The orientation filter used in Phase 3 of this project. Open source reference implementations exist on GitHub.
- Skog et al. 2010, *IEEE Transactions on Biomedical Engineering*. Zero velocity update (ZUPT) method for stride length integration without GPS or external markers.
- **Used in:** Phases 3, 4, 5.

### Timed 25 Foot Walk (T25FW)
- Cutter et al. 1999, *Brain*. The standard EDSS lower limb measure. The project's gait test does not directly replicate T25FW but produces complementary cadence and stride length numbers.

### Sloan low contrast acuity
- Balcer et al. 2003, *Neurology*. Validation of low contrast acuity at 100, 25, 5, and 1.25 percent for tracking optic neuritis recovery and progression in MS. The contrast levels in `SPEC.md` Section 6.3 come directly from this.

### Symbol Digit Modalities Test (SDMT)
- Smith 1968, *Western Psychological Services*. The original paper SDMT.
- Benedict et al. 2017, *Multiple Sclerosis Journal*. Validation of digital SDMT adaptations in MS. Used to justify the 90 second duration and digital format in `SPEC.md` Section 6.4.

### Voice biomarkers in MS
- Romero-Arias T, Delgado Hernández J, Betancort M, Gálvez García G. 2025, *Journal of Voice*, "Voice Alterations in Multiple Sclerosis: A Systematic Review and Meta-analysis of Acoustic Parameters." DOI 10.1016/j.jvoice.2025.10.017. PMID 41206297. Article PII S0892199725004370. Epub ahead of print, November 7, 2025; volume, issue, and pages not yet assigned. Bibliographic detail confirmed by the Citation Auditor in the Phase 0 to Phase 1 cleanup re audit via Crossref, OpenAlex, and PubMed. MS specific systematic review and meta analysis comparing acoustic voice parameters between people with MS and healthy controls. Reports statistically significant differences in six of seven acoustic indicators with clinically relevant effect sizes, including increased jitter and shimmer (two of the three voice quality features in this project's feature set, alongside harmonics to noise ratio), increased F0 standard deviation, and decreased maximum phonation time. The review provides MS specific evidence that the jitter and shimmer features are sensitive to MS bulbar function, replacing the previously cited (and Parkinson's specific) Rusz et al. 2018 *Sleep Medicine* entry per Citation Auditor Phase 0 verdict A.15. Companion MS specific work from the same research group at the University of La Laguna (Romero-Arias et al., "Exploring Biomechanical Correlates in Voice Analysis of Multiple Sclerosis Patients," *Folia Phoniatrica et Logopaedica* 77(2):147 to 160, DOI 10.1159/000540457; and Delgado-Hernandez J, Betancort M, Romero-Arias T, Hernandez-Perez MA, "Voice analysis as a digital biomarker: A machine learning approach for automated multiple sclerosis classification," 2026 paper in press) further supports the use of acoustic voice biomarkers in MS, although those companion papers are not the primary anchor.
- Boersma P. 2001, *Glot International* 5(9/10):341 to 345, "Praat, a system for doing phonetics by computer." Standard reference for jitter, shimmer, and HNR algorithmic definitions; the Phase 8 implementation should produce features matching Praat's definitions or document the deviation explicitly.
- **Caveat from the Citation Auditor (Phase 0 to Phase 1 cleanup re audit):** bibliographic detail (DOI, PMID, full author list, publication date) is now confirmed via Crossref, OpenAlex, and PubMed. The substantive content claims (e.g., "six of seven acoustic indicators significantly differing") come from the published abstract summary in indexing services, not from the full text, because ScienceDirect returned 403 to direct fetches in the audit. Phase 8 should re verify the specific findings against the full text before this paper is referenced in any user facing artifact (README, in app About screen, PDF report). The substantive claim that jitter, shimmer, and HNR are MS specific bulbar function biomarkers is corroborated by the Romero-Arias group's other MS specific publications named above, so the Phase 8 voice feature selection rationale does not stand or fall on the systematic review citation alone.
- **Used in:** Phase 8 Voice Test feature selection.

#### 1. Bilateral Tap Test, status: approved with caveats. Clinical Validator, Phase 0, 2026-05-07.

The 30 second alternating bilateral tap, with the dominant hand performed once and the non dominant hand performed once, is a defensible smartphone proxy for the 9 Hole Peg Test in the sense that both probe upper limb dexterity and both produce a continuous quantitative output (peg completion time for 9HPT, tap rate and inter tap CV for the smartphone version). The reference for digital tap proxies in MS already in this file (Bays et al. 2015) is cited at a level of specificity (alternating bilateral tap rates correlate with 9HPT completion times) that I cannot independently confirm from this file alone. Specifically, the existing entry does not give a journal name, a DOI, or a correlation coefficient, and I will not paraphrase the strength or direction of the relationship from memory.

Caveats and required actions:

1. Tap rate alone is a coarser signal than peg time; the inter tap interval coefficient of variation, the dominant versus non dominant asymmetry, and the miss rate (already specified in SPEC.md Section 6.1) are what give the test discriminative power, not the rate. SPEC.md Section 6.1 already lists all four; no design change required, but the project should not market or chart "tap rate" as the primary feature in isolation.
2. The Floodlight Pinching Test, which is a related upper limb smartphone test, was one of the four tests that significantly discriminated MS from non MS in Oh et al. 2024 after age and sex adjustment. This is supportive evidence that smartphone upper limb motor tests are a valid axis for MS, but the Pinching Test is not the same as a bilateral alternating tap; the Citation Auditor should not let the project conflate the two.
3. The dominant first then non dominant ordering in SPEC.md Section 6.1 follows 9HPT clinical convention and is appropriate; no change.

Citations the Citation Auditor should verify in Phase 0:

1. Bays et al. 2015. The current entry in this file gives no journal, no DOI, and no specific finding beyond "alternating bilateral tap rates correlate with 9HPT completion times." The Citation Auditor needs to find the actual paper, confirm the year and venue, confirm whether it is in MS specifically or in another neurological population, and pull the actual correlation coefficients into this file. If the cited paper is in a non MS population, an MS specific replacement is required before Phase 2.
2. Mathiowetz et al. 1985. Already cited as the original 9HPT reference; the Citation Auditor should confirm the exact journal volume and page numbers for the bibliography.
3. Feys et al. 2017, *Multiple Sclerosis Journal*, the consensus paper on the 9HPT in MS clinical trials. I am flagging this as an unverified suggestion: I believe a Feys et al. consensus paper on the 9HPT exists and is widely cited in the MS literature, but I am not 100 percent certain of the year, the exact journal, or the exact title from memory, and I will not assert those without verification. The Citation Auditor should determine whether this paper exists, whether it is the correct anchor for the 9HPT in MS, and whether to add it to this file.

Patient Advocate flag: the 30 second alternating tap with both hands is low burden and low fall risk. No safety concern. The Patient Advocate should still confirm that the tap target size and on screen layout are reachable for patients with mild hand weakness or tremor, but that is a Phase 2 UI concern, not a clinical design concern.

#### 2. Gait Test, status: approved with caveats. Clinical Validator, Phase 0, 2026-05-07.

The 30 second straight line walk with the phone in the front pocket, recording at 100 Hz IMU and producing the per stride feature set (cadence, stride length, step time variability, stride asymmetry, double support time), is the right design for an MS gait module given the published evidence base. The decision to compute per stride features rather than report a global distance metric is directly supported by the Floodlight Open finding already cited in this file: the Two Minute Walk Test, which reports only total distance covered, did not significantly distinguish MS from non MS in the Oh et al. 2024 cohort after age and sex adjustment, while the U Turn Test (which captures gait quality, not gait quantity) did. The reference values for cadence and step length cited in this project (MS versus control step length 45.3 vs 72.1 cm, cadence 94.4 vs 115.2 steps per minute) are from Givon et al. 2009 (*Gait Posture* 29(1):138 to 142), per the Citation Auditor's Phase 0 finding; Comber et al. 2017 (*Gait Posture*) reports standardized mean differences in a meta analysis and is a complementary, not primary, source for these specific raw values. These are what the project's per stride features should be evaluated against in Phase 5.

Caveats and required actions:

1. Stride count adequacy at 30 seconds. Healthy walkers cadence around 100 to 120 steps per minute, which gives 50 to 60 steps in 30 seconds, or roughly 25 to 30 strides per leg. MS patients walk slower; using the Comber 2017 cadence of 94.4 steps per minute, 30 seconds yields about 47 steps total, or about 23 strides per leg. That is enough strides to compute means and coefficients of variation for cadence, step time, and stride asymmetry with reasonable stability, but it is on the lower end. The project should explicitly compute and report the number of detected strides as a quality score factor; SPEC.md Section 7.1 step 9 already lists "at least 20 steps detected" as a quality threshold, which is appropriate. No design change required, but I want this surfaced to the QA Engineer in Phase 5: any session with fewer than 20 detected strides should be excluded from feature trend calculations, not just flagged with a low quality score.
2. The Socie et al. 2014 reference (PubMed 25117855; Citation Auditor Phase 0 verdict B.8 corrected the first author from Sosnoff to Socie, with Sosnoff listed as the third author of three: Socie MJ, Motl RW, Sosnoff JJ, *International Journal of Rehabilitation Research* 37(4):311 to 316, DOI 10.1097/MRR.0000000000000074) was cited to me by the PM as evidence on spatiotemporal gait during the 6 Minute Walk Test in MS. The Citation Auditor confirmed the paper exists and characterizes spatiotemporal gait parameters in MS during the 6MWT but does not specifically validate a 30 second window as adequate. The Phase 5 validation suite will need to include a power analysis showing 30 seconds is sufficient stride count for stable feature estimation in the target population, since no published paper specifically addresses the 30 second smartphone walking window in MS.
3. The straight line constraint matters. The U Turn Test was the gait test that discriminated MS from non MS in Oh 2024; turn quality is therefore a known signal. The current SPEC.md Section 7 design captures only straight line walking, by design (cumulative yaw drift is treated as a quality score factor in step 9, meaning a non straight walk is rejected, not analyzed). I am approving this scope, because adding turn analysis would expand the Phase 3 and Phase 4 footprint substantially; future-ideas.md should record turn quality as a deferred feature, with Oh 2024 as the supporting reference.
4. SPEC.md Section 7.1 step 5 sets the maximum inter peak distance at 800 ms, which corresponds to a minimum cadence of 75 steps per minute. The Comber 2017 mean MS cadence is 94.4 steps per minute, which is comfortably above 75, but the Comber meta analysis is on a mean, not on the slowest walking MS patients in the population the application is targeting. Patients with EDSS scores in the 6 to 6.5 range (cane assisted ambulation) can walk at cadences below 75 steps per minute; the 800 ms ceiling will reject their data as "pauses or stops." This is a clinical limitation worth documenting in the Phase 5 validation report, and the Patient Advocate should be aware that the gait module as currently specified is not designed for the ambulation impaired tail of the MS population. SPEC.md Section 3 already targets users with "mild gait instability" and that scope is consistent with the 800 ms ceiling, so no design change is required, but the limitation should be made explicit in the README and the in app About screen.

Citations the Citation Auditor should verify in Phase 0:

1. Comber et al. 2017. The current entry in this file references "Comber 2017 (Gait Posture meta analysis)" with specific MS versus control step length and cadence values. The Citation Auditor should confirm the exact author list, the exact title, the journal volume and page numbers, the DOI, and verify those cited values appear in the paper as written. The values are load bearing for Phase 5 validation targets and for the Phase 1 reference range overlays, so verification matters.
2. Sosnoff et al. 2014, PubMed 25117855. Author list, title, journal, exact relevance to the 30 second window decision. The Citation Auditor should pull the abstract and confirm the paper supports what the project claims it supports.
3. Buckley et al. 2020, *Sensors*. The current entry cites a stride length error of 4 to 7 percent across published methods; the Citation Auditor should confirm the exact range and that the methods surveyed are smartphone IMU based, not motion capture or instrumented walkway based.
4. Skog et al. 2010 ZUPT reference. Confirm exact citation; the original ZUPT method has multiple foundational papers and the project should anchor on the most appropriate one for foot or hip mounted IMU. The phone in the front pocket position is closer to a hip mount than a foot mount, and the Citation Auditor should flag if the cited paper is foot mount specific (which would mean a different ZUPT formulation may be appropriate).
5. Madgwick 2010. Confirm the original paper title, the author, and the standard citation. The Madgwick filter has many cited variants; the project should anchor on the original paper if possible.

Sign off on the SPEC.md Section 7 pipeline itself: the Madgwick filter for orientation estimation, a 4th order zero phase Butterworth low pass at 20 Hz, peak finding on vertical linear acceleration with a 250 ms minimum and 800 ms maximum inter peak distance, lateral acceleration sign for left versus right pairing, and ZUPT for stride length integration are all conventional, well documented choices in the published smartphone gait literature. No design change required at the pipeline level. The Phase 5 validation suite is what proves the pipeline produces clinically meaningful numbers; that is the point at which I will sign off finally on the gait module, not now.

Patient Advocate flag: the 30 second walk requires a safe walking space. Galati et al. 2024 specifically cited "physical or contextual barriers (including unsafe locations for the gait test)" as a quit reason in the Floodlight cohort. The retention design specification already captures this (gait test contextual skip rules: defer when not in a walkable space). The Patient Advocate should review the in test instructions to ensure the safety language is unambiguous: walk in a straight line, on a flat surface, with no obstacles, with a wall or counter within reach if needed. Patients with EDSS 4 or higher should not perform the test alone if there is any risk of fall; the application should not screen out these patients (they need the most data), but should advise them to have a person nearby. This is a Phase 4 UI and Phase 11 accessibility concern, not a Phase 0 clinical concern, but I am flagging it now so it does not get lost.

#### 3. Low Contrast Vision Test, status: approved with caveats. Clinical Validator, Phase 0, 2026-05-07.

The Sloan low contrast acuity chart at 100, 25, 5, and 1.25 percent contrast is the published standard for MS optic neuritis tracking, and the existing Balcer et al. 2003 entry in this file specifically validates these four contrast levels for that purpose in MS. SPEC.md Section 6.3 matches that. Multiple choice tap input rather than voice or freehand input is appropriate for an unsupervised home test (it removes voice recognition error and removes the rater).

Caveats and required actions:

1. Letter set and chart layout. The Sloan letter set is a specific subset of the alphabet (the standard Sloan optotypes are C, D, H, K, N, O, R, S, V, Z), and the spacing, line layout, and progression rules from the original Sloan chart are part of what makes the test valid. SPEC.md Section 6.3 says "Sloan style chart" but does not specify the letter set, the line by line progression, or the scoring algorithm in detail. Before Phase 6, the PM should dispatch the Android Engineer (with Clinical Validator and Citation Auditor review) to write a short design note specifying the exact letter set, the exact contrast levels, the exact line progression, and the exact scoring rules (number of letters per line, termination criteria, total possible score). Without that note, "Sloan style" is vague enough to drift into something the literature does not actually support.
2. Display contrast accuracy. Sloan low contrast acuity is sensitive to display gamma and ambient light. The application's CameraX ambient brightness check (already specified) is necessary but not sufficient. The Phase 6 design note should address what the project commits to about display calibration (Android phones vary widely in default gamma and color profile) and what the quality score does when the display contrast cannot be guaranteed. At minimum, the test should document a known limitation: results are comparable within a single device over time, but cross device comparisons may be affected by display variation. This is an honest limitation, not a design defect.
3. Viewing distance. SPEC.md Section 6.3 lists "viewing distance (estimated from front camera face size via ML Kit face detection) within acceptable bounds" as a quality factor. This is appropriate; standard low contrast acuity testing is performed at a fixed distance (typically 2 meters or 1 meter depending on the chart variant), and a smartphone test cannot guarantee that, so a quality gate is the right approach. The Phase 6 design note should specify the target viewing distance range and the acceptable bounds.
4. Ambient brightness gate is approved as a quality factor, not as a hard rejection on borderline brightness; rejecting outright will frustrate users in normal home lighting that is slightly outside an idealized lab range. The threshold should be calibrated empirically in Phase 11.

Citations the Citation Auditor should verify in Phase 0:

1. Balcer et al. 2003, *Neurology*. Confirm exact author list, title, journal volume, page numbers, DOI, and confirm the paper validates 100, 25, 5, and 1.25 percent contrast levels for MS. The Phase 6 design note will lean heavily on this paper, so verification is load bearing.
2. Balcer et al. 2017, *Multiple Sclerosis Journal* (the agent brief notes mention a more recent Balcer review in MS). I am flagging this as unverified: I have not independently confirmed that a 2017 Balcer paper in *Mult Scler J* exists with the relevant content. The Citation Auditor should determine whether this paper exists and whether it should replace or supplement the 2003 reference. If there is a more recent consensus or guideline paper from the MS low contrast acuity literature, that paper is likely a better anchor than the 2003 paper alone.
3. The Sloan original chart reference (the Sloan letter set itself dates to Louise Sloan's mid 20th century work on letter optotypes). The Citation Auditor should confirm whether the project should cite the original Sloan paper and the 2003 Balcer adaptation, or only the Balcer adaptation. I do not have a confident citation for the original Sloan paper from memory and I will not assert one.

Patient Advocate flag: Sloan low contrast at 1.25 percent is genuinely hard for MS patients with prior optic neuritis or active inflammation; that is the point of the test. The Patient Advocate should ensure the on screen instructions and the post test feedback do not communicate the hard low contrast levels as a personal failure; the test framing should be neutral and informational.

#### 4. SDMT, status: approved with caveats. Clinical Validator, Phase 0, 2026-05-07.

The 90 second oral or written Symbol Digit Modalities Test is the most validated cognitive measure in MS, with strong test retest reliability and strong sensitivity to MS related cognitive change. The 90 second duration is the published clinical standard. SPEC.md Section 6.4 specifies a 90 second digital adaptation with a tap input on a numeric keypad against a key showing nine symbol to digit mappings; that matches the published digital SDMT adaptations in spirit.

Caveats and required actions:

1. Symbol set. This is the single most important thing the Citation Auditor and the Phase 7 design note need to nail down. The original SDMT uses a specific set of nine abstract symbols mapped to the digits 1 through 9. The validated digital adaptations use either the original symbol set (with the publisher's permission) or a published alternate set whose equivalence has been demonstrated. Using arbitrary glyphs invalidates the test. The current SPEC.md Section 6.4 does not specify which symbol set the project will use, and the implementation cannot proceed in Phase 7 without that decision being made and verified against the literature. The Citation Auditor should research what the validated digital SDMT adaptations in MS use, and whether the project needs to license the original symbol set from Western Psychological Services, or use a published alternate that is in the public domain. This is a potential blocker for Phase 7; flagging it now so the PM can plan around it.
2. Sequence randomization. The standard SDMT presents symbols in a fixed sequence on a paper form; digital adaptations randomize the sequence to prevent learning effects across repeated administrations. SPEC.md Section 6.4 does not specify whether the sequence is randomized per administration. The Phase 7 design note should commit to randomization, and the Citation Auditor should verify that randomization is consistent with the published digital adaptations.
3. Practice effects. The classic SDMT has well documented practice effects across repeated administrations; the published adaptations address this with alternate forms. A weekly self administered SDMT will run into practice effect issues unless the symbol presentation order is varied each week. The Phase 7 design note should commit to a variation strategy, and the Phase 9 reporting layer should not interpret week over week SDMT improvements as cognitive improvement without accounting for practice effects. The Clinical Outcomes Reviewer should weigh in on the trend chart wording before Phase 11.
4. The 90 second duration is the right choice and matches the published clinical standard. No design change required.

Citations the Citation Auditor should verify in Phase 0:

1. Smith 1968 (or 1973; the original SDMT manual has been published in multiple editions through Western Psychological Services). The Citation Auditor should confirm the correct citation for the original SDMT manual.
2. Benedict et al. 2017, *Multiple Sclerosis Journal*. The current entry says this paper validates digital SDMT adaptations in MS. The Citation Auditor should confirm exact citation, exact title, and confirm the paper supports the 90 second duration and the digital format specifically (some Benedict papers are on the oral SDMT in MS, which is related but not the same as a digital tap adaptation).
3. Strober et al. 2019. I am flagging this as unverified: the agent brief notes mention a Strober 2019 paper on the digital SDMT in MS, but I have not confirmed what that paper says or whether it exists in the form the brief implies. The Citation Auditor should determine whether this paper should be added to the bibliography.
4. The MSOAC (Multiple Sclerosis Outcome Assessments Consortium) has published consensus recommendations on cognitive assessment in MS that include the SDMT. I am flagging the existence of MSOAC documentation as plausible but not confirmed; the Citation Auditor should determine whether there is an MSOAC consensus citation that the project should anchor on for SDMT scope.

Patient Advocate flag: the SDMT is genuinely fatiguing for cognitively impaired MS patients, and Galati et al. 2024 captured "test burden and repetitiveness" as a Floodlight quit reason. The 90 second duration is the published standard and I am not approving a shortened version, because that would invalidate the comparison to published norms. The Patient Advocate should focus instead on the framing: the application should set expectations that the SDMT is the hardest of the five tests, should offer to skip it on a fatigued day, and should not present a low SDMT score as a cognitive decline signal without context. This is a Phase 7 UI concern and a Phase 9 reporting concern.

#### 5. Voice Reading Test, status: approved with caveats. Clinical Validator, Phase 0, 2026-05-07.

The acoustic feature set (jitter, shimmer, harmonics to noise ratio, speaking rate, pause fraction) is the canonical voice biomarker set for neurological speech analysis, and these features are widely used in the MS bulbar function literature as well as in the broader neurodegenerative voice biomarker literature (Parkinson's, ALS). SPEC.md Section 6.5's choice of these five features is defensible. A 30 second reading sample is short by clinical voice analysis standards (many published protocols use 60 to 120 seconds or more), but it is a reasonable trade off given the application's overall under 10 minute battery budget.

Caveats and required actions:

1. Reading passage choice. The current SPEC.md Section 6.5 says "a fixed 30 second passage" without specifying which one. Voice analysis reproducibility across sessions depends on every session reading the same text, so a fixed passage is the correct choice. The two most common reading passage choices in the speech pathology literature are the Rainbow Passage (a phonetically balanced English passage published by Fairbanks 1960, public domain) and the Grandfather Passage. Both are widely used and both have published norms. The Phase 8 design note should pick one and justify it. The Patient Advocate should weigh in on whether the passage is appropriate for the target audience (the Rainbow Passage is fairly long and uses some uncommon words; a shorter, simpler passage may be more appropriate for fatigued MS patients). If the project uses a custom or MS specific passage, that passage must be published or carefully constructed for phonetic balance, otherwise the acoustic features may be skewed by the specific phonetic content of the passage rather than by the speaker's voice.
2. Microphone variation across Android devices. #### Open question 1: miss rate as alternation discipline versus aim

The dispatch design summary says misses are recorded only as non alternating in target taps in v1, with off target taps not currently captured. The Patient Advocate Phase 0 framing concern 4 cautions against penalizing tremor patients for off target taps; the trade off named in the dispatch is that `miss_rate` becomes an alternation discipline metric, not an aim metric.

I want to flag a discrepancy between the dispatch design summary I was given and the Phase 2 plan at `docs/plans/phase-2-tap-test.md` line 161, which currently defines a miss as "(a) it lands outside both target hit regions, OR (b) it lands inside the same target as the previous valid tap." The plan and the dispatch summary disagree on whether off target taps are captured. Both versions are clinically defensible, but they implement different things and the choice should be explicit before Phase 2C ships. Surfacing this to the PM rather than choosing for the project: see decisions for PM review at the bottom of this note.

On the substantive clinical question, both interpretations are defensible against the SPEC.md Section 6.1 use of the phrase "miss rate," because SPEC.md does not define miss rate operationally and neither Tanigawa et al. 2017 nor Mathiowetz et al. 1985 nor Feys et al. 2017 define a smartphone "miss rate" in operational terms (the 9HPT has a different failure mode: a peg dropped or a peg not seated counts as a fault, but the task is performance time, not error rate). What I can say with literature support:

- The alternation constraint itself is part of the bilateral alternating tap construct that the Tap Test is built on. A non alternating tap is a discipline failure that is meaningful to capture, and the dispatch summary's choice to count it as a miss is consistent with the construct.
- An aim failure (an off target tap) is a separate construct (visual motor coordination plus aim) that overlaps with but is not identical to the upper limb dexterity construct anchored in Tanigawa et al. 2017. Capturing it as a miss is also defensible, but conflating it with the alternation discipline failure into a single `miss_rate` metric loses the ability to distinguish the two failure modes.

**Revision 1, required:** the `miss_rate` feature should not be a single conflated number. Whichever variant the PM and Patient Advocate settle on for v1, the persisted feature payload should keep the two failure modes distinguishable so the Phase 9 Reporting layer can present them honestly. Two acceptable options:

- Option A, capture both failure modes (Phase 2 plan line 161): persist `non_alternating_taps` and `off_target_taps` as separate integer counts in the features map, in addition to or instead of a single `miss_rate`. The roll up `miss_rate` (if kept) should be defined as `(non_alternating_taps + off_target_taps) / (valid_taps + non_alternating_taps + off_target_taps)`. This is my recommended default because it preserves the most signal and keeps both interpretations open.
- Option B, capture alternation only (dispatch summary): persist `non_alternating_taps` and explicitly rename the feature `non_alternation_rate` rather than `miss_rate` in the persisted features map, so the metric's actual meaning is honest in the schema and in the PDF report. If the project keeps the name `miss_rate` while only counting non alternating taps, downstream readers (the user's neurologist, a future clinical reviewer) will reasonably interpret it as the broader concept and be misled. Renaming is a non negotiable consequence of choosing Option B.

I prefer Option A because the alternation discipline metric is genuinely interesting (it is a coordination signal, not a tremor signal) and the off target metric is also genuinely interesting (it is an aim signal that may be confounded by tremor or visual issues). Persisting both lets the project make an informed Phase 9 reporting choice without re running anyone's data. Either option is clinically defensible if the naming is honest.

Patient Advocate framing concern 4 is honored under either option as long as the user facing copy does not communicate the count as a failure. The Patient Advocate's concern is about user facing framing, not about which counters the database holds. The schema can store both counts and still display only "Recorded with high confidence" to the user.

#### Open question 2: asymmetry index formulation

The design summary uses the symmetric mean denominator `(d - n) / mean(d, n)` rather than the simpler ratio `d / n`. I can offer the following on this:

- The symmetric mean denominator (sometimes called the symmetry index, or a Robinson style index in the gait asymmetry literature) is bounded between approximately minus 2 and plus 2 in the limit cases, is signed, and gives 0 for perfect symmetry. The simple ratio `d / n` is unbounded above, undefined when the non dominant rate is zero, and gives 1 for perfect symmetry, which is awkward for trend visualization.
- The symmetric mean denominator is the more conventional choice in gait and motor symmetry literature broadly, including in MS specific gait asymmetry analyses (see for example the stride asymmetry features in the Phase 3 to Phase 5 gait pipeline, which use a normalized ratio not a raw ratio).
- I cannot independently verify, in this pass, that the MS 9HPT or smartphone tap literature has a single canonical asymmetry formulation. The Tanigawa et al. 2017 paper uses tap rate as a primary outcome and reports finger and foot tapping separately; it does not appear to define a bilateral asymmetry index in the way this project does. The Mathiowetz et al. 1985 9HPT methodology paper uses peg completion time and does not define asymmetry between hands as a derived feature. The Feys et al. 2017 MSOAC consensus paper on the 9HPT in MS clinical trials reports dominant and non dominant times separately as primary outcomes, not as a derived asymmetry index. **The project's choice to compute and persist an asymmetry index at all is therefore a smartphone adaptation choice, not something the MS 9HPT literature mandates a specific formula for.**

Given this, the symmetric mean denominator is the right choice on general statistical grounds (bounded, signed, defined when one rate is zero, conventional in the broader symmetry literature) and is approved. The simple ratio `d / n` would not be approved.

**Revision 2, suggested but not required:** the `asymmetry_index` formula in the feature documentation and in the Phase 2 plan should explicitly cite this Phase 2 sign off note as the source of the formula choice rather than implying it is grounded in a specific MS 9HPT paper. The Phase 9 Reporting layer should present the index as "tap rate asymmetry" with a brief note that 0 is symmetric, positive means the dominant hand is faster, and a normal range is not yet established for the smartphone tap test in MS (because no MS tap test paper I am aware of reports a population asymmetry distribution).
